# GAP: Thresholds Are Western, Not Indonesian

## Missing: Empirically Calibrated Indonesian Thresholds

The Green/Yellow/Red thresholds (30%/50%) are presented as grounded in
"OJK research on sustainable saving rates" but no Indonesian empirical source
was cited in the product brief. The actual source appears to be the Western
50/30/20 budgeting rule adapted without local calibration.

## What We Know vs. What We Don't

**We know**:

- Bank Indonesia (2023): national household savings ~30-34%
- BPS SUSENAS (2022): bottom 40% negative savings
- Manning (2021): young urban professionals in Jakarta save 8-18%

**We don't know**:

- Savings rate distribution by income bracket specifically for investors
- Savings rate distribution by city (Jakarta vs Tier 2 vs Tier 3)
- Savings rate by employment type (formal vs informal vs freelance)
- How much of "savings" is informal (arisan, gold, rice) vs. formal investment

## The Calibration Gap

Even if we had real data, the thresholds solve two different problems:

1. "What savings rate is sustainable?" (behavioural)
2. "What savings rate is needed to reach this goal?" (mathematical)

The current thresholds conflate these. A 30% savings rate is "sustainable"
but may be insufficient for a 10-year property goal in Jakarta.

## What Is Needed

1. Primary research: survey of 200+ young Indonesian professionals on actual
   savings rates, informal financial obligations, and goal achievement rates
2. Calibration study: back-test threshold predictions against known outcomes
   for users who set goals 5+ years ago
3. Sensitivity analysis: show how verdict distributions shift across ±3pp
   threshold boundaries for each income bracket

## For Course Presentation

Explicitly frame current thresholds as "business judgment calibrated against
available literature" not "empirically derived from Indonesian data."
Future work: primary research with target user population.
